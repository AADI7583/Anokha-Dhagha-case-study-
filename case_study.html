Abstract

Anokha Dhaga, a women-owned social enterprise run under the umbrella of Tata Power Community Development Trust in Mumbai, did something that most community-based craft enterprises could only dream of: establishing a stable sourcing contract with Trent Ltd., owner of the Westside chain of stores. This paper studies the architecture of social capital behind this breakthrough. Based on field study in 2025-2026, the analysis highlights five sequentially generated forms of social capital that together led to the Trent order: bonding capital resulting from joint production of a crisis within a collectivity; bridging capital generated by a broker between two disjunctive institutional domains; cognitive capital emerging from a designer's six months of relational investment with the community of women; linking capital realised through official MSME accreditation and social-capital-infused credit; and finally coordination capital generated by an interstate hub-and-spoke production network. It finds that none of the capitals was independently sufficient for reaching the goal and each was dependent upon the earlier one.

Section One

Introduction

The literature on social capital has proven quite analytically convincingly that trust, networks, and norms are generative forces in economic life. Less analytically convincingly, however, the study of social capital helps explain, step by step, how communities build these relationships and networks from nothing, face repeated failures, learn from their mistakes, and slowly achieve outcomes that once seemed impossible. This case study tries to explain that process.

Anokha Dhaga is a brand featuring a federation of self-help groups of women, numerous MSMEs, and a network of production centres throughout Mumbai and beyond. Anokha Dhaga is operated by Tata Power Community Development Trust and employs women working in low-income urban informal settlements such as Ashok Nagar, Vishnu Nagar, Dharavi, and Malwani. The Anokha Dhaga enterprise makes use of waste fabric known locally as 'katran' or 'chindi' as well as the skills of its women in crafting.

The focus of this case study is on one milestone: the establishment of a sustained supply relationship with Trent Ltd., the owner of the Westside chain, whereby thousands of handcrafted insects and tiaras in waste fabrics were supplied and subsequently sold through Westside stores and tiaras used in Wesness run across India. This milestone did not come about solely through product quality. Rather, it came about through three years of deliberate effort in creating social capital: bonding, bridging, cognitive, linking, and coordination capital, each being used as the foundation for building the next type.

Every time the Anokha Dhaga journey reached another phase, a relationship made the difference in moving to the next phase. And these were many relationships, among women, among organisations, and among sectors. This was truly the secret behind this enterprise reaching a national retailer.

The vocabulary provided by social capital theory as per the theories of Bourdieu (1986), Coleman (1988, 1990), Putnam (2000), Burt (1992, 2004), Woolcock (1998), and Nahapiet & Ghoshal (1998) is capable of capturing that story analytically.

Section Two

Organisational Context and Background

Anokha Dhaga, therefore, is not a singular entity. It is an ecosystem. At its core lies Saheli, the coordinating body, responsible for the production process, managing finances, and possessing the institutional memory of the wider network. Sangeeta is in charge of Anokha Dhaga as a brand and the coordinating body Saheli, while Ganesh Sir manages the projects across all levels in the process. Anjali Tai oversees Swamini, one of the fourteen Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises that were incorporated by name in the network under women's names. The network aims to establish itself as a model for independent MSMEs where women will have formal entities as property. In that sense, the MSME registrations in this case, as explained by Bhagya Shree, are anticipatory investments for later independence.

The women behind Anokha Dhaga are mostly from communities where formal employment, institutional reach, and socio-economic mobility were denied and largely inaccessible. The fact is that they entered with practical skills such as stitching, crocheting, embroidery, assembling soft toys, etc. Still, according to Bourdieu (1986), social capital becomes valuable when the people or networks someone is connected to hold influence, power, or a strong position in society. The stronger and more respected the network, the greater the benefits a person can gain from it. Dense, highly trusted network of capable women from Ashok Nagar has social capital in its world; still, it does not necessarily imply entry into corporate procurement systems. Anokha Dhaga is needed precisely to create a connection.

The pre-requisite step before even thinking about the Trent relationship was an internal development stage that consisted in creating a strong collective identity and mutual trust through crisis sharing. As soon as the COVID-19 lock-down started in 2020, Ganesh Sir approached Anjali Tai and other women who was working with a group of women from Ashok Nagar. Many women stepped up themselves for extra income as many males of the households during that period lost their jobs. The question was straightforward – did they know how to produce masks? Fabric was acquired through DEHAT Foundation in Nagpur, additional machinery was introduced, and women from different settlements joined the process. Within a few days there were ten of them; within weeks, a hundred of women. Over two years, five lakh masks were produced, with individual earnings reaching Rs. 20,000 per month.

The masks produced income. More importantly, they helped in building social capital that Coleman (1988) describes as crucial for its operation, closed networks that ensure interdependence, multiple contacts, and the establishment of reciprocity norms based on practice and not on declarations. According to Leana & Van Buren (1999), the key aspects of organisational social capital are associated ability and trust, both achieved here by women through joint participation in the crisis, working in pressure, together meeting daily at a common place, sharing lives with each other, sharing their problems and pain, building teamwork, and helping each other.

The masks built something more important than income. They built the bonding capital that every subsequent form of social capital would depend on.

3.1 Bridging Capital: The Structural Hole and the Broker

No matter how strong Anokha Dhaga's internal bonds, there was no route into Trent's institutional world from within the women's existing network. Between Anokha Dhaga's community production ecosystem and Trent's corporate retail system lay what Burt (1992, 2004) calls a structural hole: a gap between two groups who would benefit from connection yet remain entirely unconnected. Mr. Jha and Mr. Ganesh bridged it.

As a consultant for Tata Power who had been involved in Trent's institutional culture for several years before making the move, Mr. Jha was familiar with the value that each side offered. He knew the importance that Trent placed on superior finishing, seasonal design conformity, compliance with children's safety regulations, and penalties for non-adherence to delivery schedules. However, he was also aware of the authentic handmade craftsmanship, production capability within the community, and the story of social engagement which could be provided by Anokha Dhaga. He was able to articulate both sides since he had experienced life in both.

According to Burt's (1992) theory, brokers gain their advantage from being the only link between two sides. The actual trigger for such a broker was an informal meeting where Omashang, the head designer of Trent, who is responsible for most of the collections made by Trent, admired the tote bags of Anokha Dhaga. Besides, the role played by Tata Power's corporate support cannot be underestimated. According to Putnam (2000), generalised reciprocity within a common institutional environment generates a tendency to make an investment into a relationship without guaranteeing returns. The family bond between Trent and the Tata Power Community Development Trust created this tendency precisely.

3.2 Cognitive and Relational Capital: The Designer as Translator

Bridging capital opens the institutional door; it does not, on its own, create the shared language through which two very different institutional worlds can produce something together. That required a third form of social capital, cognitive and relational, and the person who built it was Kaushiki Akhade.

Kaushiki came from JJ School of Arts with a background in texture and interior design and prior experience working with tribal artisans, PWD craftspeople, and self-help groups. Nahapiet and Ghoshal (1998) argue that the cognitive dimension of social capital, shared codes, language, and mental models, is what enables knowledge combination and the creation of new value through collaboration. Before Kaushiki's arrival, Anokha Dhaga and Trent had no shared cognitive framework. Trent's language was premium, seasonal, and Gen-Z oriented, Anokha Dhaga's language was handcraft, local inspiration, and material availability. Kaushiki's role was to build the shared cognitive infrastructure through which both vocabularies could converge on a marketable product.

It took six months of purposeful relation-building for Kaushiki. Kaushiki spent time with the women, knew their capabilities and limitations, and knew when pressure worked and when it broke down the collaborative process. She was a collaborator whose design strategy worked around the strength of every individual woman, and not a designer giving directives to production labourers.

This is where Coleman's (1990) concept of "credit slip" comes into play. Six months of consistent, respectful engagement built up trust that can be cashed in when the situation calls for pressure to be applied.

The single-day sample challenge was perhaps the most informative instance of the period. Trent allocated just a day. The team had to procure materials and create four sample pieces by 5:00 PM. The materials arrived at 11:30 AM. The women then went to the market, tried out wiring techniques and crocheting methods, and brought back four viable samples in just an afternoon. As Watson and Papamarcos (2002) reveal, discretionary effort in a situation like that is driven by trust in leadership, quality of communication, and normative congruence. All three were in full force in the afternoon session. Trent was duly impressed. That was indeed the turning point when order commitment became possible.

The single-day challenge was not a test of creative talent alone. It was a test of the social infrastructure that would have to sustain production if a real order was placed.

The two products that emerged from three years of iterative development, handcrafted insect toys made from waste chindi priced at Rs. 2,000 per fish and Rs. 500 per bug, and fabric tiaras hand-assembled from individually cut and stitched petals, solved the problem that had stalled earlier samples. Trent's existing vendors could not replicate them. The handmade quality was not a limitation; it was the product's defining aesthetic, inseparable from the human labour and craft knowledge embedded in its production. As Dess and Sauerwald (2014) rightly observe, human capital and social capital reinforce each other in this case: the human capital came in terms of the women's skill in handicrafts while the social capital infrastructure provided the means to turn it into Rs. 2,000 pieces.

3.3 Linking Capital: Swamini, Saheli, and the Institutional Threshold

Despite this successful product validation, a structural obstacle still existed. Trent needed an official vendor, which entailed MSME registration, GST number, bank account in the entity's name, and processes to process purchase orders through procurement procedures. As stated by Woolcock (1998), the link between social capital, or the ability to access and operate within formal systems, is a key element missing in most poverty reduction strategies. Community social capital could be developed in abundance, yet structural exclusion from formal structures that define their future economic development is a reality. The creation of Swamini, an independent MSME with Anjali Tai being its leader, was the step that crossed this line.

The finance involved in executing this order represents an innovative mechanism of social capital-based financing without any conventional banking equivalent. In this case, the timing mismatch between the cost incurred and the payment cycle at Trent represented a structural problem. Saheli provided Swamini with an advance of Rs. 30,000 to cover all the expenses related to account opening, materials and interim wages. The guarantee for this money was not collateral, but rather relational social capital and Saheli's faith in Anjali Tai's managerial competence. According to Coleman (1988), this function may be considered credit slip, with social capital becoming financial resources for its holder.

Every insect toy manufactured contained a QR code referencing the location where it had been made with as many as six different codes per every product showing six locations where production had taken place. Szreter and Woolcock (2004) state that linked social capital becomes crucial for women or marginalized groups since formal institutional systems tend to exclude them. This QR code was indicative of such an inclusion, as women's contributions were now officially recorded and legible. The profit distribution model further emphasised the importance of this capital transfer, as five to ten per cent of the order's profit was distributed among women outside wages. Thus, social capital was translated into financial capital resulting in ownership share (Bourdieu, 1986).

3.4 Coordination Capital: The Hub-and-Spoke Network

The fifth type of social capital was the form that allowed scaling up. The hub-and-spoke pattern used by Anokha Dhaga can be called social capital structurally: a network purposely built for the flow of trust, information and production to follow relational links designed in advance. The hub possessed design information, quality standards and client relations. The spokes included centres like Vishnu Nagar, Bhivpuri, Malwani, the Urja centre, Ghatkoparz centre, Dharavi as well as centres in other states and they had the capability to manufacture. When Trent ordered their first batch of 3,000 units, it was divided evenly into two lots: 1,200 pieces were manufactured at the central centre and 1,800 batches were dispatched to other centres. Fifty-one women managed to produce 1,200 units within six days.

It is thanks to the Training-of-Trainer method that the dissemination of information between various centres occurs with perfect accuracy. For instance, Kaushiki trains Shabnam, Shabnam teaches a group of women from Dharavi, and one thousand tiaras are manufactured within three days. Such an arrangement exists because there is always a flow of trust along established relational links. Shabnam does not get certified as an instructor, but she proves her competencies through joint manufacturing. Thus, Coleman's (1988) closure mechanism works well in this situation where dense networks among the women provide efficient dissemination of information.

Figure 1  ·  Hub-and-Spoke Production Network

Nine production centres across Mumbai, Maharashtra, and Karnataka — coordinated without a single central production floor

Nine centres were trained online and on ground, some in a single group session and some through trainer-of-trainer model, for the tiara order. Each tiara required nine hand-cut, hand-stitched fabric flowers, each assembled from five individually cut petals with a pearl added and a cord wired through the assembled crown. Three thousand tiaras required 27,000 flowers and 135,000 individually cut petals, across nine centres, in seven days. The entire chain converged across Mumbai, Karnataka, and beyond, without a single central production floor and without formal production management. Krackhardt and Hanson (1993) argue that informal networks are the real infrastructure through which organisations function under pressure. The tiara production was pure informal network operation, and it delivered.

The Karjat group that stayed in Mumbai for a week during the rainy season working until 8 or 9 p.m. on order-heavy days, three days, one thousand tiaras, crafted by women who were making the item for the first time. That's what social capital does when it's at work.

The relationship with Trent helped generate outcomes on three levels. Firstly, at institutional level, it produced several registered MSMEs, and gave them visibility in market, which is a replicable model and generated revenue of Rs. 32 lakh and Rs. 14 lakh distributed as profit to the women. Trent's procurement system, with it child safety testing requirements, QR traceability standards, formal invoicing, helped upgrade and set a higher quality infrastructure at Anokha Dhagha, which now governs the production across different clients. Nonakha and Taekuchi (1997) describes this as converting relational knowledge into organisational knowledge, experience instilled into the system and norms that will continue to persist beyond the individuals who first generated them.

At the individual level, the transformation is visible in majorly two trajectories. Shabnam entered the project as a part-time stitcher with no formal institutional connections, the journey with Trent gave her spatial mobility as she has since after gaining experience travelled to Nagpur, Gujarat, Bangalor, and Delhi, and trained different groups and shared her knowledge across centrs. Now she works with sixty women, communicates deadlines, and delegates the work across centres and coordinates with the hub through relational authority that was earned through demonstrated competence.

At the collective level, the women describe something that the accounts and books cannot fully capture. Shabnam's account of seeing Anokha Dhaga's products displayed in a Westside mall shows it very precisely, it was easy for them to shop in a mall, and they were happy that the product they had made was displayed in the stores. Bourdieu (1986) theorises this as symbolic capital, the idea of symbolic capital means that when these women saw their work displayed in a respected retail store, it gave them recognition and respect. It made them feel like real and legitimate business producers. Adler and Kwon (2002) note that social capital has intrinsic value, which means that relationships, trust, and belonging have value on their own, not just because they help make money. For the women of Anokha Dhaga, the friendship, teamwork, shared goal, and pride of seeing their craft sold in a national store were valuable in themselves. These benefits cannot be measured only by revenue.

5
Section Five

Discussion

Trent's journey is a perfect fit for the stepwise dependence outlined in the literature on social capital. The bonding capital served as the motivational and practical basis that made all other types of social capital possible. The bridging capital provided the institutional gateway that made it possible to appreciate the product. The cognitive capital supplied the shared vocabulary that made bridging connections possible. The linking capital provided the legal and economic basis that made the institutional transaction possible. Coordination capital ensured the required scale such that a supply relationship with one firm was enough to ensure the profitability of the business.

There is an important point to be made from this structural perspective. As explained by Hawkins and Maurer (2012), in the same spirit as DeFilippis (2001), strong bonding capital among people in low-resource settings will not guarantee upward mobility. Without the brokerage provided by Mr. Jha, there would not have been a structural link to Trent. Without the institutional support of Tata Power, there would not have been a legitimacy signal. Without the social capital credit of Saheli, there would not have been working capital. And without the translation of Kaushiki, there would not have been a product capable of being understood in the corporate market. All these inputs came from outside the women's current network. This is a structural observation about what is possible when external bridging and linking resources meet up with internal bonding capability.

The main structural risk is dependency on a single corporate buyer. The entrepreneur in question clearly recognizes this risk and sees diversification of markets as a strategic necessity. The appropriate response is precisely what Burt's (1992) network theory says it should be: diversification of linking connections through developing more varied products and having many customers. The second risk follows from Coleman's (1988) concept of depletion: the trust and solidarity needed to create discretionary effort can diminish over time if their burden is too high.

6
Section Six

Conclusion

Trent order was not the result of product development but the consequence of social capital building – consciously constructed, sequentially built, and continuously maintained over the course of three years of attempts, failures, refinements, and investments of trust. All the five types of capital – bonding, bridging, cognitive, linking, and coordination – were indispensable but individually insufficient. The mutual dependence of the above is the key learning point of this case study.

For practitioners of CSR-driven business development and community-based economic enterprises, this is an example of an approach to be replicated rather than a recipe that can be mechanically applied. Firstly, invest in creating bonding capital through a common crisis or a common goal. Secondly, facilitate brokerage through establishing legitimacy and proving capabilities. Thirdly, build the institutional structure of linking capital before market necessity dictates improvisation.

Indeed, the women who produced handcrafts for Westside at the Anokha Dhaga enterprise did much more than merely produce their handcrafts for Westside. They constructed the networks that enabled their craft to be recognized and their business to take an institutional form through the process of doing visible labor.
